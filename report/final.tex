\section{Introduction}

Breast cancer remains one of the leading causes of cancer-related mortality among women in Canada, and early detection is a central factor in improving patient outcomes \cite{ref:example5}. Although mortality has declined substantially over recent decades, radiology workflows remain constrained by the growing volume of screening and diagnostic imaging \cite{ref:example5}. In practice, radiologists review two-dimensional mammograms acquired from multiple views and assess suspicious regions such as masses and micro-calcifications before downstream treatment decisions can be made. This creates a clinically important bottleneck: delays in expert review can delay triage and treatment initiation, especially for patients with potentially malignant lesions \cite{ref:bleicher2016}.

\medskip \noindent
This project seeks to determine whether a relatively simple neural-network pipeline, trained on curated mammography data, reliably identify malignant abnormalities and support faster prioritization of high-risk cases. Beyond binary malignancy classification, we also consider whether the same framework can provide clinically useful secondary outputs, including abnormality type (mass versus calcification) and BI-RADS-informed risk stratification. Because BI-RADS categories are designed to communicate the probability of malignancy and recommended follow-up actions, they provide a natural target for machine-learning-assisted prioritization in a real clinical queue \cite{ref:example4}.

\medskip \noindent
To address this question, we use the CBIS-DDSM dataset, a curated subset of the DDSM archive released through The Cancer Imaging Archive \cite{ref:example1}. The dataset includes mammography images, lesion annotations, pathology labels, and associated metadata that support both classification and lesion-focused preprocessing. CBIS-DDSM was selected because it is a widely used benchmark for breast-cancer imaging studies, enabling direct comparison with prior literature, including the shallow-CNN and transfer-learning baselines reported by Das et al. \cite{ref:example2}.

\medskip \noindent
The goals of this study are threefold. First, construct a reproducible preprocessing pipeline that converts heterogeneous raw files into standardized model-ready inputs. Second, train and evaluate a neural-network classifier for malignant-versus-benign prediction and compare its performance with published benchmarks. Third, explore extended prediction tasks relevant to clinical triage, specifically lesion-type identification and BI-RADS-related scoring, to assess whether the model can provide richer decision support than a binary label alone.




\section{Methods}

\subsection{Dataset}
The original DDSM (Digital Database for Screening Mammography) dataset on the Cancer Imaging Archives (CIA) created contains thousands of "normal" cases with no tumors and no masks. The CBIS-DDSM (Curated Breast Imaging Subset of DDSM) is a modernized, cleaned-up subset of that original database currated by Lee et al. \cite{ref:example1} 

\medskip
\noindent
Labelled data for this collection is structured as a ROI (Region of Interest) image that shows an identified abnormaly. A patient's entire mammogram does not have a unique label and as such each participant has multiple patient IDs multiple abnormalities of either of the two categories for calcification and mass abnormalites or both, store in their respective CSV files \cite{ref:example1}.

\subsection{Preprocessing}

The preprocessing pipeline was designed to convert the raw CBIS-DDSM metadata and image files into a consistent training table for both classification and mask-aware learning. First, the dataset builder combines the mass and calcification case-description CSV files with the DICOM metadata file and matches each sample using a composite key built from the abnormality type, patient ID, breast side, image view, and abnormality ID. This step links the cropped ROI image, the full mammogram, and the corresponding ROI mask whenever they are available.

\medskip
\noindent
To standardize the input data for the machine learning models. Every sample was loaded and resized to a fixed resolution of $224\times224$ pixels customary to many Shallow CNNs and ResNet architectures \cite{ref:example3}. ROI images are read in grayscale.

\medskip
\noindent
Images were normalized using the ImageNet-style statistics $(\mu=0.485,\, \sigma=0.229)$, following standard ImageNet-pretrained computer-vision pipeline \cite{ref:example3}. An active augmentation pipeline applies horizontal flips $(p=0.5)$, vertical flips $(p=0.3)$, and random brightness/contrast perturbations $(p=0.5)$. Rotations, elastic deformations, and grid distortions were tested in development but were disabled in the final transform configuration. These augmentations improve robustness to orientation and intensity variation while preserving the alignment between each mammogram crop and its mask. The final output of preprocessing is therefore a structured master dataset containing the abnormality image, it's mask, pathology label, BI-RADS score, and abnormality type for each sample as shown in Figure \ref{fig:samplese}.

\subsection{Pathology Classification}
The primary classification task is to predict the pathological status of each abnormality, which is a binary classification problem. Considering the baseline performance reported by Das et al. \cite{ref:example2} for a two-layer ShallowCNN, we implemented a similar architecture and trained it on the preprocessed dataset first. We also implemented a three-layer ShallowCNN with dropout to evaluate whether a slightly deeper architecture with regularization could improve performance.

\medskip \noindent
After a stratified 5-fold cross-validation was performed with the initial model, the dataset was split into training, validation, and test sets using an 80-10-10 split. The training set was used to fit the model parameters, while the validation set was used for hyperparameter tuning and early stopping. The test set was reserved for final performance evaluation.

\subsection{BI-RADS Score Prediction}
The certainty of how likely a lesion is malignant can help add priority to whether or not a
scan should be reviewed immediately. In this section, the neural network was adjusted to give images
a BI-RADS score to rank the probability of a malignant lesion and determine if they are
malignant based off of this score. The BI-RADS score is a standardized system used by radiologists to categorize breast imaging findings based on their level of suspicion for malignancy. The categories range from 0 to 5, with higher scores indicating a greater likelihood of malignancy. For this task, the model was trained to predict the BI-RADS score for each abnormality, which can provide more nuanced information about the risk level of the lesion compared to a binary classification.

\medskip \noindent
BI-RADS scores are typically assigned with the following interpretations:
\begin{itemize}
    % make the list more compact in spacing
    \setlength\itemsep{.01\smallskipamount}
    \item BI-RADS 0: Incomplete - Additional imaging evaluation needed
    \item BI-RADS 1: Negative - No findings of concern
    \item BI-RADS 2: Benign - Benign with no suspicion of malignancy
    \item BI-RADS 3: Probably Benign - Probably benign but short-term follow-up is recommended
    \item BI-RADS 4: Suspicious - Suspicious for malignancy and biopsy should be considered
    \item BI-RADS 5: Highly Suggestive of Malignancy
    \item BI-RADS 6: Known Biopsy-Proven Malignancy 
\end{itemize}


\medskip \noindent
The distribution of BI-RADS scores in the dataset is shown in Figure \ref{fig:dists} (c) with BI-RADS 6 excluded as it is not applicable for mammography prediction and it requires prior biopsy results. Given that the dataset only contains three samples with BI-RADS score of 1, the models were trained on a dataset where BI-RADS scores of 0 and 1 were grouped together.

\subsubsection{CNN with Categorical Crossentropy}
For the BI-RADS score prediction task, a CNN architecture similar to the three-layer ShallowCNN was used, but with modifications to accommodate the multi-class classification problem. The output layer was changed to have 5 neurons corresponding to the BI-RADS categories (0/1, 2, 3, 4, 5), and a softmax activation function was applied to produce probability distributions over the classes. 

\medskip \noindent
The model was trained using categorical cross-entropy loss and evaluated using the same metrics of accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score for each BI-RADS category. However, the metrics were calculated using a macro-average approach to account for class imbalance in the dataset, especially given the low number of samples in certain BI-RADS categories.


\subsubsection{Regressor with Mean Squared Error Loss}
Given that the BI-RADS score is an ordinal variable, another approach was to treat the prediction task as a regression problem. In this case, the output layer of the CNN was modified to have a single neuron with a linear activation function, and the model was trained using mean squared error (MSE) loss. The predicted BI-RADS score was then rounded to the nearest integer to assign a category. This approach allows the model to capture the ordinal nature of the BI-RADS scores, potentially improving performance by leveraging the inherent ordering of the categories.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Pathology Classification}
The results of the pathological classification task indicate that both the two-layer and three-layer ShallowCNN models were able to achieve reasonable performance in distinguishing between malignant and benign abnormalities in the CBIS-DDSM dataset. The three layer ShallowCNN with dropout showed a slightly higher accuracy and recall compared to the two-layer model, suggesting that the deeper architecture may have been better at identifying malignant cases. However, this came at the cost of lower precision, indicating that the three-layer model also produced more false positives. 

\medskip \noindent
Our three-layer ShallowCNN with dropout marginally outperformed the reference base models in work by Das et al. \cite{ref:example2} with an accuracy of 0.82 compared to their reported accuracy of 0.78 for a model with two convolutional layers and no dropout. This improvement may be attributed to the additional convolutional layer and the use of dropout for regularization, which can help prevent overfitting and improve generalization to unseen data. However, the performance of both models is still limited by the relatively simple architecture and the complexity of the task, suggesting that more sophisticated models may be needed to achieve higher accuracy in pathological classification of mammography images.

\medskip \noindent
The confusion matrices for both models revealed that the majority of misclassifications were false positives, particularly for the two-layer ShallowCNN. This suggests that while the models were able to identify many malignant cases, they also struggled to correctly classify benign cases, which could lead to unnecessary follow-up procedures in a clinical setting. The three-layer model with dropout showed a better balance between precision and recall, but further improvements may be needed to reduce false positives while maintaining high sensitivity for malignant cases.


\subsection{BI-RADS Score Prediction}
Our results showed that a simple CNN model struggled to accurately predict the BI-RADS scores, with the categorical model performing slightly worse than the regression model. The confusion matrices for both models revealed significant misclassifications across all BI-RADS categories, particularly for the BI-RADS 4 category, which is clinically important for identifying suspicious lesions that may require biopsy.

\medskip \noindent
The regression model showed fewer misclassifications between distant categories compared to the categorical model, suggesting that treating the BI-RADS score as an ordinal variable may have helped capture the underlying structure of the data better than the categorical approach. However, overall performance was still limited, indicating that further model development and potentially additional data may be needed to improve BI-RADS score prediction accuracy.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}
Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged. First, the two pathological classification models were relatively simple ShallowCNN architectures. While the three-layer model with dropout marginally outproformed the reference base models in work by Das et al. \cite{ref:example2}, literature shows that  more complex architectures such as ResNets \cite{ref:example2} or DenseNets \cite{ref:densenet} could potentially achieve better performance, albeit with increased computational requirements and risk of overfitting given the dataset size. Second, the BI-RADS score prediction task proved to be more challenging than binary classification, likely due to the increased complexity of predicting multiple classes and the ordinal nature of the BI-RADS scores. Future work could explore more sophisticated CNN models with regression approaches for BI-RADS prediction, such as ordinal regression models or multi-task learning frameworks that jointly predict pathology and BI-RADS scores. 

\medskip \noindent
Additionally, segmentation models such as the pre-trained YOLO models \cite{ref:yolo} that leverage the ROI masks provided in the dataset could be developed to focus on lesion-specific features, which can improve both pathological classification and BI-RADS score prediction. Due to time constraints, this was not explored in the current study, but it represents a promising avenue for future work on such a dataset which has segmentation masks available.


\section{Conclusion}

In this study, we developed and evaluated relatively simple neural-network pipelines to determine their capacity for both the binary pathological classification and BI-RADS-informed risk stratification of mammographic abnormalities using the CBIS-DDSM dataset. Our findings indicate that a three-layer ShallowCNN architecture can effectively learn to distinguish malignant from benign lesions, achieving a peak accuracy of 0.82 and marginally outperforming baseline shallow models. The extended task of directly predicting multi-class, ordinal BI-RADS scores proved markedly more difficult, emphasizing the inherent challenges of automated ordinal clinical scoring on non-uniform, imbalanced datasets. While our current models lay the fundamental framework for using machine-learning tools to streamline radiological triage queues, they also underscore the need for employing significantly more advanced architectures, such as pre-trained complex CNNs and segmentation models with much larger cohorts, to meet the rigor and precision required for clinical deployment.
