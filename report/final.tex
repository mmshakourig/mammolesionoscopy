\begin{abstract}
This experiment investigated the fundamental principles of optical cavities, specifically the
requirements for resonance and key properties such as finesse, free spectral range, and cavity linewidth. Using a
knife-edge technique, we experimentally determined the Gaussian beam parameters, such as the
beam radius to be $w=0.2808 \pm 0.006$ mm. Calculations for ideal mode-matching into the cavity were performed, and the
cavity was setup accordingly. Using a HeNe laser and a piezoelectric (PZT) scanning stage,
we characterized cavity performance for lengths between 4 cm to 20 cm. These results were
compared to theoretical values derived from mirror reflectivities measured with a power meter.
While we achieved a maximum experimental finesse of $262.0 \pm 10$, experimental values were consistently above the theoretical prediction of $207.9 \pm 15.8$, likely due to uncertainties in the mirror reflectivity measurements and minor misalignments. Measured cavity linewidth were also smaller than the theoretical predictions. Stability
analysis confirmed a cavity length limit at $L\approx30$ cm, beyond which transmitted power
significantly decreased. These findings highlight the importance of the geometric setup and
alignment of an optical resonator.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Introduction}
To optimally couple or "mode match" a laser beam into the TEM$_{00}$ mode of an optical cavity, the spatial profile of the input beam must closely match the cavity's fundamental mode. This requires that the beam waist of the input laser be located at the position of one of the cavity mirror M1 and that its size be such that the beam radius at M2 matches the cavity mode's beam radius at that location. To achieve this, we measured the beam radius of the input laser using a knife-edge scan and two different approaches.


% \vspace{-.5\baselineskip}
\subsection{Methodology}

A knife-edge scan was performed to measure the beam radius of the laser. The knife-edge was moved across the beam in small increments, and the power of the transmitted light was measured at each position. Figure \ref{fig:rn} (left) shows the normalized measured intensity as a function of the knife-edge position. Given the Gaussian nature of the beam, the beam waist can be estimated using the positions where the intensity is 10\% and 90\% of the maximum. The beam waist is then calculated using the relation $w = 0.78 (x_{90} - x_{10})$, where $x_{90}$ and $x_{10}$ are the positions corresponding to 90\% and 10\% intensity, respectively.

\medskip
\noindent
An alternative method to estimate the beam waist is to fit the entire knife-edge transmission curve to the theoretical integrated Gaussian model. Since the transmitted power beyond the knife edge corresponds to the cumulative integral of a Gaussian beam up to edge position $x_k$, the normalized intensity is given by:

\begin{equation}
    \bar{I}(x_k)=\frac{1}{2}\,\operatorname{erfc}\!\left(\frac{\sqrt{2}\,(x_k-x_0)}{w}\right)
    \label{equation:erfc}
\end{equation}

% \vspace{-.5\baselineskip}
\subsection{Results}
From the plot, we can interpolate the positions where the intensity is 10\% and 90\% of the maximum, which correspond to $x_{90} = 12.375 \pm 0.055$ mm and
$x_{10} = 12.015 \pm 0.0059$ with the uncertainties being estimated as half of the step size of the knife-edge movement.

$$
w = 0.78 (x_{90} - x_{10}) = \mathbf{0.2808 \pm 0.006} \text{ mm}
$$


\noindent
In Figure, \ref{fig:rn} (right), a non-linear least-squares fit was applied with MATLAB to the full data, and the parameter $w$ was obtained from the fit. From the MATLAB results, the beam waist was found:
\[
w = \mathbf{0.2803 \pm 0.0005} \text{ mm}.
\]
Because this approach uses the full profile of the knife-edge scan, it is less sensitive to local noise and small fluctuations, giving a result that is very consistent with, but more reliable than, the two-point estimate in Figure \ref{fig:rn} (left).

\subsection{Mirror Reflectivity}


\noindent \textcolor{red}{(A) } Using a power meter sensor and having removed the ND filters, we measured the power of the incident, reflected, and transmitted beams of each of the M1 and M2 mirrors in isolation. To ensure minimal scattering losses and accurate readings, we minimized the angle of incidense when making measurements. From these measuremnts, the reflectivity and transmission coefficients of each mirror was calculated according to $R = P_\text{reflected}/P_\text{incident}$ and $T = P_\text{transmitted}/P_\text{incident}$. For both of the mirrors, the coefficients do not add up to unity exactly. In theory, they should sum up exactly to 1; however, minor losses in our setup from scattering and absorption cause the experimental results to not exactly add up to 1. Table \ref{table:mirror} summarizes our measurements and the results. The uncertainties in the power measurements were estimated based on the stability of the meter readings where the error is the half of the range of the fluctuations in the readings.

\noindent \textcolor{red}{(B) }
The expected finess of the cavity is calculated using the results in \textcolor{red}{(A)} as follows:

\begin{equation}
    F = \frac{\pi\sqrt{r}}{1-r} = \frac{\pi\sqrt{\sqrt{R_{M1}R_{M2}}}}{1-\sqrt{R_{M1}R_{M2}}} = \mathbf{207.9 \pm 15.8}
\end{equation}

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(C) }
Knowing the wavelength of the laser $\lambda=632.8$ nm, speed of light $c=3\times10^8$ m/s, and $n_\text{air}=1.0003$ \cite{saleh_teich}, the expected free spectral range, $v_\text{FSR}$, and the cavity linewidth, $v_\text{FWHM}$, of the cavity with length $L=0.15$ m can be calculated as follows:


$$ 
v_\text{FSR} = \frac{c}{2nL} = \frac{3\times10^8}{2\times1.0003\times0.15} = 999.7 \text{MHz} 
$$

$$ v_\text{FWHM} = \frac{v_\text{FSR}}{F} = \frac{632.8}{207.9} = 4.81 \text{MHz} $$

\noindent
We can convert these from frequency to spactial domain using the relation $v = c/\lambda$ :

$$
L_\text{FWHM} = \frac{\lambda}{2F} = \frac{632.8}{2\times207.9} =  1.53 \text{nm}
$$

$$
\lambda_\text{FWHM} = \frac{\lambda}{F} = \frac{632.8}{207.9} = 3.04 \text{nm}
$$
Since we don't have control over the frequency of the laser and it is fixed, a better measure
for our cavity resonances may be the cavity linewidth in nm, as this we could adjust by changing
the cavity length.


\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(D) }
If the mirrors of the cavity get dirty, the reflectivity of the mirrors will decrease, which will lead to a decrease in the finesse of the cavity. As a result, the cavity will have a broader linewidth or an increased $v_\text{FWHM}$. This means that any cavity resonance would be less sharp and more broad, making it harder to differentiate between different cavity modes.

\noindent \textcolor{red}{(C) }
For a gaussian beam focused by a lens with focal length f, the beam waist at the focal length
can be found with Eq. (6.73) in \cite{saleh_teich} as follows:

\begin{equation}
w_0 = \frac{2\lambda f}{\pi D}
\end{equation}
where D is the lens diameter. In this case D = 1.2mm since the beam is localized to a
diameter of 1.2mm, and f = 50cm. The beam waist is then:

$$
w_0 = \frac{2\times632.8\times10^{-6}\times0.5}{\pi\times1.2\times10^{-3}} = \mathbf{0.168} \text{ mm}
$$


\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(D) }
The experimental beam waist of 0.2803 mm is significantly larger than the theoretical value of 0.168 mm. This disagreement is largely attributable to the assumption that the laser emits a perfectly collimated Gaussian beam right at the aperture; in reality, the beam experiences some slight divergence before it reaches the 50 cm mode-matching lens. Additionally, subtle alignment imperfections or spherical aberrations in the lens can slightly distort the focal spot size, contributing to the experimental difference.

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{blue}{(i) }
The beam waist of the output HeNe laser beam was measured using the knife-edge method as described in part \textcolor{red}{(B)}. Figure \ref{fig:2i} shows the knife-edge scan and the fit to the complimentary error function model in Equation \ref{equation:erfc}. The beam waist was found to be $\approx$ \textbf{0.494 mm}, which yields a smaller laser diameter than the theoretical value used in part \textcolor{red}{(C)}. This discrepancy can be attributed to factors such as beam divergence and lens imperfections, as discussed in part \textcolor{red}{(D)} and explains why our measured beam waist at the focus is larger than the theoretical value calculated in part \textcolor{red}{(C)}. Using the newly measured beam diameter of $D_{\text{meas}} = 2\times0.494=0.988$ mm, the expected beam waist is much closer to the experimental value:

$$
w_0 = \frac{2\lambda f}{\pi D_{\text{meas}}} = \frac{2\times632.8\times10^{-6}\times0.5}{\pi\times0.988\times10^{-3}} = \mathbf{0.204} \text{ mm}
$$

\subsection{Mode Matching}
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(A) }
Figure \ref{fig:beam_phase} shows a sketch of the beam shape and phase fronts inside the cavity. To couple into the cavity
the beam must meet boundary conditions at M1 and M2. These conditions are that the beam radius of curvature should match that of both M1 and M2 at these locations respectively.
Since at M1, the beam must have $R(M1) = \infty$, the beam waist should also be located at M1.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figs/3a.png}
    \caption{Sketch of the beam phase fronts inside the cavity.}
    \label{fig:beam_phase}
\end{figure}

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(B) }
The HeNe beam should come to a focus at the location of M1 since M1 best matches the beam waist location. Such a beam will satisfy the boundary condition of $R(M1) = \infty$.


\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(C) }
The beam waist needed at M1 to best mode match the cavity can be calculated with $z=L=15$ cm using the
following equations:

\begin{equation}
    R_2 = R(L) = L\left[ 1 + (\frac{z_0}{L})^2 \right] = 30 \text{ cm}
\end{equation}
This gives the Reyleigh width of the beam as
\begin{equation}
    z_0 = \sqrt{L(R_2-L)} = 15 \text{ cm}
\end{equation}
Thus, the beam waist needed at M1 can be calculated as
\begin{equation}
    w_0 = \sqrt{\frac{\lambda z_0}{\pi}} = 0.1738 \text{ mm}
\end{equation}

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(D) }
Using the ideal beam waist calculated in (C), the required focal length of the mode-matching lens can be calculated using the focusing equation:
\begin{equation}
    f = \frac{\pi w_0 w_\text{in}}{\lambda} = \frac{\pi \times 0.1738 \times 0.6}{632.8 \times 10^{-6}} = 518 \text{ mm}
\end{equation}
To have the focus at M1, the lens should be placed at a distance $d=f$ away
from the M1 mirror.


\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(E) }
When only the flat mirror is present in the beam path, it acts as a simple attenuator. The intensity of the transmitted light is reduced. However, because the mirror is optically flat, it does not introduce any focusing effects. The phase fronts and the divergence of the transmitted light remain the same as the incident beam. Thus, the beam profile and mode structure of the transmitted light are preserved, albeit at a reduced intensity.

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(F) }
When the second mirror is introduced and is properly aligned, it forms a cavity, significantly increasing the beam's intensity. The system becomes highly sensitive to the spatial mode structure of the input beam with phase fronts that match the cavity's resonant modes. The cavity supports specific resonant modes, and only those modes that match the cavity's boundary conditions will be efficiently transmitted. Thus, the net transmission becomes highly sensitive to both the cavity length and the spatial misalignment. 

\subsection{Cavity Scanning}

\noindent \textcolor{red}{(A) }
Prior to initiating the PZT scan with the cavity properly aligned, the transmitted beam observed on the Raspberry Pi camera exhibits rapid switching among various cavity modes, a behavior resulting from minor cavity length fluctuations due to mechanical vibrations in the uncontrolled setup. Once the PZT scan commences, the beam stabilizes significantly on the camera display, revealing the fundamental cavity mode. This stabilization occurs because the PZT modulates the cavity length at a sufficiently high rate that the observed transmission represents a time-averaged signal across multiple cavity lengths, effectively filtering out higher-order modes. Correspondingly, the photodiode voltage signal on the oscilloscope exhibits well-defined periodic resonance peaks, each representing a point where the cavity achieves resonance with distinct modes as the cavity length varies throughout the PZT scan.

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(B) }
Using the PZT datasheet, we assume the displacement is approximately linear with applied voltage. The datasheet indicates a displacement of 17.4\,\si{\micro m} at 150\,V and 11.6\,\si{\micro m} at 100\,V. From these values, the proportionality constant is

\[
\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta V} = \frac{17.4 - 11.6}{150 - 100}
= 0.116\,\si{\micro m/V}.
\]
Therefore, the voltage change required to shift the PZT by \(\lambda/2\) is
\[
\Delta V = \frac{\lambda/2}{0.116\,\si{\micro m/V}}
= \frac{316.4\,\si{nm}}{0.116\,\si{\micro m/V}}
\approx 2.73\,\si{V}.
\]

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(C) }
Figure \ref{fig:4c} presents the transmission spectra for cavity lengths of 5\,cm and 20\,cm over one full piezo-voltage sweep. Although one free spectral range in both cases still corresponds to a physical cavity-length change of $\lambda/2$, the modal content within that range is noticeably different. In the longer cavity, the larger accumulated Gouy phase shifts the resonance positions of higher-order transverse modes relative to the fundamental TEM$_{00}$ mode. Thus, the peak spacing and grouping of transverse modes differ between the two traces. The coupling strength of particular higher-order modes also changes because the beam spot size at M2 is different for each cavity length.


\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{blue}{(i) }
The PZT was calibrated by applying the known voltage sweep and counting the number of modes observed on the oscilloscope, which correspond to the number of times the cavity length changes by \(\lambda/2\) during the voltage sweep. The P2P voltage change applied in scans of Figure \ref{fig:4c} is 20\,V since the PZT driver has a gain of x10. There are 9 peaks, or changes in cavity length of \(\lambda/2\), observed in the 20\,cm cavity scan. This corresponds to a change in voltage of $20/9 = 2.22$\,V per \(\lambda/2\) change, which is close to the expected value of 2.73\,V calculated in part \textcolor{red}{(B)}.

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(C) }
Rather than a single transmission maximum, a series of peaks appeared at regular intervals because the input laser beam was not perfectly matched to the cavity's fundamental mode. Consequently, the incident light excited a combination of several transverse cavity modes, each resonating at a slightly different cavity length.
Of the observed peaks, the fundamental TEM$_{00}$ mode was the most intense since the HeNe laser beam itself is approximately TEM$_{00}$. As a result, the spatial overlap between the input beam and the cavity's fundamental mode was much greater than for the higher-order transverse modes.

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(D) }
To achieve resonance in the cavity, the round-trip phase shift of the light must be an integer multiple of $2\pi$. 
The resonance frequency for a specific TEM$_{lm}$ mode is given in the lab manual [Milner(2025)] as:
\begin{equation}
    \nu_{q,l,m} = v_{FSR}\left[q + \frac{\Delta \xi}{\pi}(l+m+1)\right]
\end{equation}
where $q$ is the longitudinal mode index, and $l$ and $m$ are the transverse mode indices. 
Because higher-order modes accumulate a different total phase per round trip compared to a plane wave, they act as though they traverse slightly different diagonal trajectories and experience a different effective cavity length. Consequently, a fixed-frequency laser will excite different modes at slightly different physical cavity lengths.

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{blue}{(i) }
A greater number of transverse modes were clearly observed in the longer cavity. This behavior is closely related to the beam radius at the curved mirror (M2). As the cavity length L increases toward the mirror curvature radius $R_2$ near the edge of stability, the beam spot size at M2 grows substantially.


\subsection{Cavitty Finesse}

\noindent \textcolor{red}{(A) }
The cavity finesse was estimated by measuring the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the resonance peaks in the transmission spectrum for multiple cavity lengths. The period of the peaks, $t_\text{period}$, was measured by counting the time between consecutive peaks of the same mode in the oscilloscope trace, which corresponds to a change in cavity length of $\lambda/2$. The FWHM, $t_\text{FWHM}$, was measured by zooming in on a single resonance peak and determining the time interval between the points where the transmission is half of the maximum. 
The results are summarized in Table \ref{table:finesse} and an example of the measurements for the 4 cm cavity is shown in Figure \ref{fig:fwhm}. The uncertainties in the period and FWHM measurements were estimated based on the resolution of the oscilloscope and the stability of the signal, with the error being half of the smallest time division on the oscilloscope for $t_\text{period}$ and half of the range of fluctuations in the signal for $t_\text{FWHM}$.

\noindent \textcolor{red}{(B) }
We know that L changes by $\lambda/2$ in $t_\text{period}$, we can use this to determine the speed at which the cavity length changes, then calculate the distance traveled in $t_\text{FWHM}$, which corresponds
to $L_\text{FWHM}$.
The finesse was then calculated using the relation $F = t_\text{period}/t_\text{FWHM}$. Table \ref{table:finesse} also includes the calculated finesses for different cavity lengths.

\noindent
In Figure \ref{fig:6b}, we observe that the finesse values calculated are close but generally above the expected finesse value of 207.9 we calculated based on the mirror reflectivities.
This is likely due to the fact that our cavity is not very well aligned, and there are more
losses in our system that cause the cavity modes to be more broad. Theoretically, the finesse
should not depend on cavity length, however we see that the finesse values do change with
cavity length. These discrepencies are likely due to the missalignment of the cavity as we changed the
length, which causes the losses in the cavity to change and the finesse to change as
well.

\noindent \textcolor{red}{(C) }
Unlike Finesse, the free spectral range in the frequency domain is dependent on the length of
the cavity. Using the experimentally determineed finesse values, the cavity linewidth in the frequency domain can be calculated as $v_\text{FWHM} = v_\text{FSR}/F$. The results are shown in Figure \ref{fig:6c} where the experimentally derived cavity linewidth and the theoretical expected linewidth calculated using the theoretical finesse as a function of cavity length are plotted. The experimental linewidths follow the theoretical trend closely but the experimental
values are generally slightly lower than the theoretical predictions which is likely due to misalignment and extra losses in the cavity.

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(D) } One possible reason that a smaller linewidth than expected in Figure \ref{fig:6c} could be because the HeNe laser we are using is not perfect and outputs light at multiple frequencies close to
632.8nm. This means when we measure the cavity linewidth, it will appear smaller than it sholud be in theory.


\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{blue}{(i) }
The cavity quality factor $Q$ is defined as the ratio of the resonance frequency \(\nu_q\) to the resonance linewidth \(\nu_{\mathrm{FWHM}}\):
\begin{equation}
Q=\frac{\nu_q}{\nu_{\mathrm{FWHM}}}
=\frac{c/\lambda}{\nu_{\mathrm{FWHM}}}
=F\,\frac{2nL}{\lambda}
\end{equation}

\noindent
Using the measured finesse for the 20 cm cavity, $F=211.2$, the estimated quality factor is
$$
Q=211.2\times\frac{2(1.0003)(0.20\,\text{m})}{632.8\times10^{-9}\,\text{m}}={1.34\times10^{8}}
$$


\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{blue}{(ii) }
If a \(440\ \text{Hz}\) tuning fork had this same \(Q\)-factor, the \(1/e\) amplitude decay time would be
\begin{equation}
\tau=\frac{Q}{\pi f}
=\frac{1.34\times10^{8}}{\pi(440\,\text{Hz})}
=9.71\times10^{4}\ \text{s} \approx 27\ \text{hours}.
\end{equation}
This is a very long decay time and represents an idealized estimate. In a real tuning fork, extra damping effects (e.g., air drag and internal material losses) would shorten the ringing time substantially.

\subsection{Cavity Stability}
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(A) } The stability of a two-mirror cavity is set by the mirror curvatures and cavity length.  
For a resonator, the stability criterion is
\begin{equation}
0 \le \left(1-\frac{L}{R_1}\right)\left(1-\frac{L}{R_2}\right) \le 1.
\end{equation}

\noindent
In our setup, \(M_1\) is flat (\(R_1=\infty\)) and \(M_2\) is concave with \(R_2=30\,\text{cm}\). So, the condition becomes:
$$
0 \le 1\cdot\left(1-\frac{L}{30\,\text{cm}}\right)\le 1
\;\Rightarrow\;
0 \le L \le 30\,\text{cm}.
$$
Hence, the maximum stable cavity length for this mirror configuration is \(30\,\text{cm}\).

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(B) } Cavity tranmission spectra were scanned near the stability limit as the cavity length was increased from 29.3 cm to 30.2 cm, approaching and surpassing the stability limit. As we approached the stability limit, we observed that width of the mode peaks in the photodiode voltage signal began to widen slightly, meaning the linewidth of the cavity was increasing. This intuitively makes sense as when the caivty approaches instability, the geometry of the cavity no longer can stop all of the light from escaping, and so the finesse and effective reflectivity of the cavity decrease.
This decrease in finesse comes with an increase in linewidth. Figure \ref{fig:7a} shows the transmission spectra for different lengths where the increase in linewidth is clearly visible as we approach the stability limit. Above the stability limit, the cavity modes disappear entirely and the transmission spectrum becomes almost flat as shown in the 30.2 cm trace.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figs/7a.png}
    \caption{Transmission spectra for cavity lengths near the stability limit, showing the increase in linewidth as the cavity approaches instability.}
    \label{fig:7a}
\end{figure}

\medskip
\noindent \textcolor{red}{(C) } As we apprach the limit of stability, the transmitted power of the cavity to the photodiode
decreases significantly. This can be observed in Figure \ref{fig:7a} purple trace for the 30.3 cm length. Once we pass the stability limit, the transmitted power becomes very
small and we can no longer see clear cavity modes in the photodiode signal anymore. Any
small peaks are indistinguishable from noise. This is because once we pass the stability limit,
most of the light escapes, and it is to the same effect as not having a cavity at all.
